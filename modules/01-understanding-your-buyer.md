# Module 01: Understanding Your Buyer

## The Brisbane SMB Buyer Profile

The typical Sun State Digital customer is a Brisbane or South-East Queensland small business owner:

- **Trade-based:** Plumbers, electricians, builders, landscapers, HVAC techs, cleaners
- **Health and professional services:** Dentists, physios, chiropractors, lawyers, accountants
- **Hospitality and retail:** Cafes, restaurants, boutique shops, beauty salons
- **Owner-operated:** The person answering your WhatsApp IS the boss

### Typical Profile
- Age: 30-55
- Revenue: 200K-2M per year
- Employees: 1-10 (often just themselves)
- Tech comfort: Low to moderate - uses iPhone, Facebook, maybe Instagram
- Marketing knowledge: Limited - they know they need to be on Google but not how
- Time: Scarce - they are busy doing the work, not thinking about marketing

---

## What They Actually Care About

They do NOT care about:
- Search engine optimisation
- Digital marketing strategy
- Brand awareness
- Impressions and reach
- Website design trends

They DO care about:
- More phone calls
- More bookings
- More jobs on the calendar
- Not losing work to the competitor down the road
- Not wasting money on things that do not work
- Looking professional when someone Googles them

Key insight: Never say digital marketing to a tradie. Say more jobs from Google.

---

## Pain Points - What Keeps Them Up at Night

### 1. Invisible Online
Their Google Business Profile is incomplete, their website has not been updated since 2018 or does not exist, and they are losing enquiries every day to someone who invested in their online presence.

### 2. Losing Jobs to Competitors
This is personal. They built their business through reputation and referrals, and now a competitor is undercutting them through Google. This pain is your strongest entry point.

### 3. Wasted Ad Spend
Most small businesses have been burned by agencies that set up ads and disappeared. Acknowledge this pain. It is a real objection and a real opportunity to differentiate.

### 4. The DIY Trap
They have something that looks like a web presence but it is doing nothing for them. They are not even sure if it exists properly. This creates an opening - low bar to clear, high perceived value when you show them what good looks like.

### 5. No Time
This is not really an objection - it is a signal that the solution needs to be EASY. Your job is to make the process feel effortless: You do not have to do anything. Give me 10 minutes and we handle everything.

---

## Decision Triggers - What Makes Them Act

### 1. Peer Recommendation
My mate Dave uses these guys and his phone has not stopped since. The most powerful trigger. Social proof from someone in their industry or local area is gold.

### 2. Google Search Moment
They Googled their own business name or their competitor and got a rude shock. When they are in this state, close fast.

### 3. Seasonal Pressure
Summer is coming and I need to be ready. Timing your outreach to seasonal pressure creates relevance.

### 4. A Competitor Win They Know About
Competitive intelligence triggers action. If you know a competitor of your prospect is dominating online, mention it tactfully.

### 5. Price/Value Certainty
They have been quoted 5,000-15,000 by other agencies and walked away. When you present 199 and make the value tangible, they feel like they would be stupid to say no.

---

## Buyer Psychology: Trust First, Price Second

Brisbane business owners are skeptical. They do not buy on logic alone. They buy on trust.

The trust ladder:
1. They feel heard - you asked about THEIR business
2. They feel understood - you identified their specific problem
3. They feel confident - you have proof it works
4. They feel safe - no lock-in, no risk, easy to start
5. THEN they think about price

If you lead with price, you skip all of this. If you build through this sequence, price becomes almost irrelevant.

### The Like Factor
People buy from people they like. This is especially true in Brisbane where the culture is friendly and relationship-driven. Be warm, genuine, and slightly casual without being unprofessional.

Target tone: Like a mate who happens to know a lot about getting businesses found online. Not a salesperson. A trusted advisor who happens to have a solution.

## The Buying Journey: Where Are They?

### Stage 1: Awareness
They know they have a problem but have not decided to act.

Signs: I know I need a website. I should probably sort out my Google listing. I have been meaning to do this.

Your approach: Educate and validate. Confirm the problem is real and worth solving. Do not pitch yet.

Example: Yeah, it is really common. What tends to happen is that people are searching right now for someone like you, and if they cannot find you, they find your competitor. How many jobs do you think you are missing?

### Stage 2: Consideration
They have decided to act but are weighing up options.

Signs: I have spoken to a couple of agencies. I was looking at doing this myself. My mate suggested Wix.

Your approach: Differentiate. Help them see why SSD is the right choice. Focus on speed, simplicity, price certainty, and local expertise.

### Stage 3: Decision
They are ready to buy and just need to feel certain.

Signs: How does it work? What do I need to do? Can I see an example?

Your approach: Remove friction. Make the next step simple. Assumptive close.

Example: So the next step is just getting your domain locked in. Are you happy with businessname.com.au or do you want to check a few options?

---

## Practical Application

Before every sales conversation ask yourself:
1. What industry is this person in?
2. What is their likely pain point right now?
3. Where are they in the buying journey?
4. What success story can I reference from a similar business?
5. What is the one outcome that would make this a no-brainer for them?
